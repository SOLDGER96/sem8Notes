### **Core Characteristics of Social Media**

To truly understand social media, it is best to look at the core characteristics that set it apart from conventional, traditional media. These properties are essential in creating a collaborative online ecosystem. 

Here are the 8 core characteristics of social media:

**1. Social Media is Many-to-Many**
* Social media enables interaction among users in a many-to-many fashion[cite: 1339]. 
* This is vastly different from conventional technological media like print, radio, television, and telephone, which generally operate on a one-to-many or one-to-one basis.

**2. Social Media is Participatory**
* Unlike conventional technologies, social media actively encourages user participation and feedback. 
* Users can engage in online discourse through activities like blogging, leaving comments, tagging, and sharing content.

**3. Social Media is User-Owned (Content)**
* While the platforms themselves are provided by large corporations (such as Meta/Facebook or Google), the actual content is generated, owned, and controlled by the social media users. 
* Without this user-generated content and active involvement, social media platforms would just be empty, boring online spaces.

**4. Social Media is Conversational**
* It stands out from traditional media not just because of the ease of conversation, but because of its many-to-many conversation abilities. 
* These characteristics make it possible for the masses to communicate and collaborate in real-time.

**5. Social Media Enables Openness**
* By utilizing Web 2.0 channels, social media provides completely new opportunities and pathways to access data and information openly.

**6. Social Media Enables Mass Collaboration**
* Social media channels allow the masses to collaborate in a many-to-many fashion to successfully achieve shared goals.

**7. Social Media is Relationship-Oriented**
* The majority of social media tools allow users to easily establish, build, and maintain both social and professional relationships or ties. 
* Some tools (like Facebook) focus primarily on personal relationships, while others (like Twitter or LinkedIn) lean toward professional relationships.

**8. Social Media is Free and Easy to Use**
* Being free of cost and extremely easy to use are two of the primary reasons social media has proliferated so rapidly and widely in the digital space.

### **Explain Tie-Strength in Social Media Network Structure**

**1. Introduction: What is a Tie?**
In Social Network Analysis (SNA), a network consists of **nodes** (users/actors) and **edges** (the connections between them). An edge connecting two nodes is referred to as a **tie**. A tie represents a relationship or interaction between two entities on a social media platform (e.g., being \"friends\" on Facebook, \"following\" on Twitter, or connecting on LinkedIn).

**2. What is Tie-Strength?**
Tie-strength refers to the **intensity, closeness, or depth of the relationship** between two nodes in a network. Not all connections in a social network are equal; some are deep and personal, while others are superficial and casual. 

The concept was famously formalized by sociologist Mark Granovetter in his 1973 paper, *\"The Strength of Weak Ties.\"* According to Granovetter, tie-strength is a combination of four dimensions:
* **Amount of time:** How much time the users spend interacting.
* **Emotional intensity:** The depth of emotional connection.
* **Intimacy (mutual confiding):** The level of trust and sharing of private information.
* **Reciprocal services:** The mutual exchange of favors or interactions (e.g., liking and commenting on each other's posts).


**3. Types of Ties based on Strength**
In social media analytics, ties are generally categorized into three main types:

* **A. Strong Ties:**
    * **Definition:** These are close, frequent, and deeply connected relationships (e.g., family members, close friends, or tight-knit colleagues).
    * **Characteristics in Social Media:** Users with strong ties frequently interact, tag each other in photos, send direct messages, and share similar interests (a concept known as *homophily*).
    * **Network Impact:** Strong ties tend to form dense, tightly knit clusters or cliques. While they offer high trust, they often share **redundant information** because everyone in the clique knows the same things and accesses the same news feeds.

* **B. Weak Ties:**
    * **Definition:** These are loose, infrequent connections, typically representing acquaintances, distant colleagues, or \"friends of friends.\"
    * **Characteristics in Social Media:** Users might follow each other or be connected on LinkedIn but rarely interact directly. 
    * **Network Impact (The Strength of Weak Ties):** Weak ties are incredibly valuable in network structure because they act as **bridges** between different dense clusters. They are the primary pathways for **novel information, new ideas, and viral content** to spread across different segments of a network that would otherwise remain isolated.

* **C. Latent / Absent Ties:**
    * **Latent Ties:** These are potential connections that exist structurally (e.g., two people who work at the same company and are on the same platform) but have not yet been activated by any interaction. Social media algorithms often suggest these as \"People You May Know.\"
    * **Absent Ties:** Ties that do not exist or represent mere \"nodding\" relationships with no significant interaction.

**4. Significance of Tie-Strength in Social Media Analytics**
Understanding tie-strength is crucial for businesses, marketers, and platform developers:
* **Feed Algorithms:** Platforms like Facebook and Instagram calculate tie-strength based on user interactions (likes, comments, profile visits) to determine which posts appear at the top of a user's newsfeed. Content from strong ties is prioritized.
* **Viral Marketing & Information Diffusion:** Marketers looking to make a campaign go \"viral\" target nodes with many weak ties, as these act as bridges to broadcast the message to diverse, unconnected groups. 
* **Recommendation Systems:** Platforms analyze weak and latent ties to suggest new friends, pages, or groups to users, helping to continuously expand the network. 

**Summary for Exams:**
Tie-strength measures the closeness of a relationship. While **strong ties** provide trust and network cohesion within small groups, **weak ties** act as critical bridges that allow fresh information and viral content to flow across the broader social media network.

### **The Seven Layers of Social Media Analytics**

**Introduction:**
Social media, at a minimum, consists of seven layers of data. Each of these layers carries potentially valuable information and insights that organizations can harvest for business intelligence purposes. While some of these layers are highly visible and easily identifiable (such as text and user actions), others are invisible (such as underlying social media and hyperlink networks). 

The seven layers of social media analytics are:

**1. Text Analytics (Layer One)**
* **Explanation:** Social media text analytics deals with the extraction and analysis of business insights from the textual elements of social media content. This includes unstructured data such as comments, tweets, blog posts, and Facebook status updates. Text analytics is predominantly used to understand the sentiments of social media users or to identify emerging themes and topics.
* **Common Tools:** Discovertext, Lexalytics, Tweet Archivist, Twitonomy, Netlytic, LIWC, and Voyant.

**2. Network Analytics (Layer Two)**
* **Explanation:** Social media network analytics focuses on extracting, analyzing, and interpreting personal and professional social networks. Examples of these networks include Facebook, Friendship Networks, and Twitter. The main goal of network analytics is to identify influential nodes (such as key people and organizations) and determine their structural position within the network.
* **Common Tools:** NodeXL, UCINET, Pajek, Netminer, Flocker, Reach, and Mentionmapp.

**3. Actions Analytics (Layer Three)**
* **Explanation:** This layer deals with extracting, analyzing, and interpreting the specific actions performed by social media users. These actions include likes, dislikes, shares, mentions, and endorsements. Actions analytics is heavily relied upon by marketers to measure the popularity, influence, and predictive value of content in social media.
* **Common Tools:** Lithium, Twitonomy, Google Analytics, and SocialMediaMineR.

**4. Mobile Analytics (Layer Four)**
* **Explanation:** Mobile analytics is considered the next frontier in the social business landscape. This layer specifically deals with measuring and optimizing user engagement through mobile applications (apps).
* **Common Tools:** Countly, Mixpanel, and Google Mobile Analytics.

**5. Hyperlink Analytics (Layer Five)**
* **Explanation:** Hyperlink analytics is about extracting, analyzing, and interpreting social media hyperlinks, specifically focusing on in-links and out-links. Analyzing these hyperlinks can reveal critical information such as Internet traffic patterns and the specific sources of incoming or outgoing traffic to and from a target source.
* **Common Tools:** Webometrics Analyst and VOSON.

**6. Location Analytics (Layer Six)**
* **Explanation:** Also known as spatial analysis or geospatial analytics, location analytics is concerned with mining and mapping the physical or geographical locations of social media users, their contents, and associated data. 
* **Common Tools:** Google Fusion Table, Tweepsmap, Trendsmap, Followerwonk, Esri Maps, and Agos.

**7. Search Engine Analytics (Layer Seven)**
* **Explanation:** Search engine analytics focuses on analyzing historical search data to gain valuable insights into a wide range of areas[cite: 1576]. This includes conducting trends analysis, keyword monitoring, analyzing search result histories, and evaluating advertisement spending statistics.
* **Common Tools:** Google Trends.

### **Challenges in Social Media Analytics**

**Introduction:**
While social media analytics offers immense value to businesses by providing real-time consumer insights, the process of extracting, cleaning, and analyzing this data is fraught with difficulties. Social media data is fundamentally different from traditional business data, leading to several technical, analytical, and ethical challenges.

Here are the key challenges in Social Media Analytics:

**1. The 3 Vs of Big Data (Volume, Velocity, and Variety)**
* **Volume:** Social media platforms generate exabytes of data daily. Storing, processing, and analyzing this massive scale of data requires expensive infrastructure and advanced big data technologies (like Hadoop or Spark).
* **Velocity:** Data is generated at an unprecedented speed (real-time tweets, live streams, instant comments). Capturing and analyzing this data in real-time to make split-second business decisions is highly complex.
* **Variety:** Unlike traditional databases, social media data is highly unstructured and comes in multiple formats (text, images, GIFs, videos, audio, emojis, and hashtags). Building algorithms that can process all these formats simultaneously is a major hurdle.

**2. Veracity (Data Quality and Noise)**
* Not all data on social media is useful or accurate. Platforms are filled with **bots, spam, fake news, and paid trolls**. 
* Separating genuine consumer sentiment from automated noise (veracity) is a significant challenge for analysts. If the input data is flawed, the resulting business insights will be misleading.

**3. Context, Sarcasm, and Nuance in NLP**
* Text analytics relies heavily on Natural Language Processing (NLP). However, human language on social media is informal, chaotic, and context-heavy.
* **Sarcasm and Irony:** A user might tweet, *\"Brilliant, my flight is delayed by 5 hours again! Best airline ever.\"* A basic sentiment analysis tool might classify \"Brilliant\" and \"Best\" as positive, completely missing the sarcasm.
* **Slang and Multilingualism:** Users frequently use abbreviations (LOL, BRB), local slang, misspelled words, or mix multiple languages in a single sentence (e.g., \"Hinglish\"), making it difficult for standard algorithms to parse the meaning.

**4. Data Privacy and Ethical Concerns**
* With massive data leaks (like the Cambridge Analytica scandal) and strict data protection laws (such as GDPR in Europe and CCPA in California), collecting user data has become legally complex.
* Analysts must ensure they are fully compliant with privacy laws, obtaining user consent where necessary, and anonymizing personal identifiable information (PII) before analysis.

**5. API Restrictions and Platform Boundaries**
* Social media data is owned by third-party corporations (Meta, X/Twitter, LinkedIn). Analysts rely on Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) to scrape or collect this data.
* These platforms frequently change their API policies, restrict the amount of data that can be downloaded, or charge exorbitant fees for enterprise-level data access. This limits the scope of data an analyst can gather.

**6. Integration with Traditional Business Data**
* For social media insights to be truly actionable, they must be integrated with traditional business data (like CRM data, sales figures, and supply chain metrics).
* Because social data is unstructured and boundary-less, mapping a social media profile to a specific customer in a company's CRM database is incredibly difficult. 

**7. Short Shelf-Life of Data**
* Social media trends change rapidly. What is viral today might be completely forgotten tomorrow. By the time analysts clean the data, run the models, and generate reports, the insight may already be outdated. 

**Summary for Exams:**
The main challenges in social media analytics revolve around handling the **sheer scale and messy nature of unstructured data (Volume, Velocity, Variety)**, filtering out **noise/spam (Veracity)**, teaching machines to understand **sarcasm and context**, overcoming **API limitations**, and strictly adhering to **global privacy laws**.

### **The Social Media Analytics Cycle**

**Introduction:**
Social media analytics is a six-step iterative process that involves both the \"science\" and \"art\" of mining desired business insights from social media data[cite: 1583]. At the very center of this analytics cycle are the **Business Objectives**, which inform and guide every single step of the journey[cite: 1584]. The process continues until these stated business objectives are fully satisfied. 

The six stages of the Social Media Analytics Cycle are:

**Step 1: Identification (The \"Art\" of Analytics)**
* This initial stage is concerned with searching and identifying the right source of information for analytical purposes.
* Data for analysis can be harvested from both business-owned platforms and nonofficial social media platforms.
* The source and type of data selected must be strictly aligned with the core business objectives.
* Framing the right question and knowing exactly what data to analyze is extremely crucial for gaining useful business insights.

**Step 2: Extraction (The \"Science\" of Analytics)**
* Once a reliable and minable source of data is identified, the data must be extracted.
* The method and tools suitable for extraction are determined by the type of data (e.g., text, numerical, or network) and its size.
* Extraction can be done manually for small-size numerical information (like counting Facebook likes), or via an API (Application Programming Interface) for large-scale automated extraction.
* **Crucial Consideration:** This stage must strictly consider privacy and ethical issues; data extraction practices should never violate a user's privacy, and organizations are advised to craft their own social media privacy policies to govern data handling.

**Step 3: Cleaning**
* This step involves removing unwanted or irrelevant data from the automatically extracted datasets.
* While some data can go directly into analysis, other data (especially text) requires heavy cleaning. 
* For text analytics, cleaning involves using Natural Language Processing (NLP) to filter, code, and cluster data to get rid of irrelevant textual noise.
* This coding and filtering process can be automated by machines or performed manually by human coders.

**Step 4: Analyzing**
* At this stage, the cleaned data is analyzed to extract meaningful business insights without losing the data's integrity.
* The steps and approach taken here will vary greatly depending on the specific layer of social media analytics being considered and the algorithms employed.
* Having strong background knowledge and an understanding of the specific analytical tools and their capabilities is crucial to arriving at the correct answers.

**Step 5: Visualization**
* In addition to numerical results, most social media analytics will result in visual outcomes. 
* Effective visualization (Visual Analytics) is highly beneficial for complex and huge data because it reveals hidden patterns, relationships, and trends that numbers alone cannot show.
* Different types of data yield different visualizations, such as:
    * **Network data (With whom):** Shows who is connected to whom (e.g., Twitter following networks).
    * **Topical data (What):** Shows what themes are frequently occurring (e.g., text clouds).
    * **Temporal data (When):** Reveals longitudinal trends and patterns over time.
    * **Geospatial data (Where):** Used to map and locate data, people, and resources geographically.

**Step 6: Interpretation (The \"Art\" of Analytics)**
* The final step involves translating the analytical and visual results into a meaningful business problem solution.
* This step heavily relies on human judgment, domain knowledge, and expertise to correctly interpret valuable knowledge from the data.
* Organizations can improve this stage by training data scientists to produce easily consumable interactive visual results, and by improving management's ability to consume and understand these analytics.

### **Tie Strength and Trust in Social Networks**

**1. Understanding Tie Strength**
In Social Network Analysis (SNA), a network is made up of nodes (users) and ties (the connections between them). **Tie strength** refers to the intensity, closeness, and depth of the relationship between two users. It is generally measured by factors such as the frequency of interaction, emotional intensity, intimacy, and reciprocal services (mutual favors).

Ties are broadly categorized into:
* **Strong Ties:** Close friends, family members, and tight-knit colleagues.
* **Weak Ties:** Acquaintances, distant colleagues, or \"friends of friends.\"

**2. The Relationship Between Tie Strength and Trust**
There is a direct and powerful correlation between tie strength and trust within a network:
* **High Trust in Strong Ties:** Strong ties are built on a foundation of high trust, emotional support, and mutual confiding. In social media marketing, users are highly likely to trust a product recommendation, review, or shared article if it comes from a strong tie. However, because strong ties usually form dense clusters (cliques) where everyone knows each other, the information circulating within these groups tends to be **redundant** (everyone already knows the same things).
* **Low Trust in Weak Ties:** Weak ties generally involve lower levels of trust and emotional investment. A user is less likely to blindly trust a recommendation from an acquaintance compared to a close friend.

### **The Significance of Weak Ties in a Social Network**

While strong ties provide trust and cohesion, **weak ties are structurally the most important connections in a social network.** This concept was famously established by sociologist Mark Granovetter in his theory, *\"The Strength of Weak Ties.\"*

The significance of weak ties can be explained through the following key points:

**1. Acting as Crucial \"Bridges\" (Spanning Structural Holes)**
Strong ties tend to form isolated, dense clusters or \"cliques.\" Without weak ties, these cliques would be completely disconnected from the rest of the network. Weak ties act as the critical **bridges** that connect these disparate, isolated groups together, creating a unified global network. 

**2. Diffusion of Novel Information**
Because people in your strong-tie cluster usually read the same news, follow the same pages, and know the same people, they rarely introduce entirely new information. Weak ties, however, connect you to different clusters with different interests and knowledge pools. Therefore, weak ties are the primary source of **novel information, new ideas, and diverse perspectives**.

**3. Viral Marketing and Content Spread**
For social media marketers, weak ties are the holy grail of content diffusion. If a piece of content only circulates within a dense cluster of strong ties, its reach is severely limited. For a campaign, meme, or hashtag to go \"viral,\" it must cross the bridges created by weak ties to reach entirely new audiences and demographic clusters.

**4. Access to Opportunities**
In professional social networks like LinkedIn, weak ties are significantly more valuable than strong ties for finding new opportunities. A close friend (strong tie) likely knows about the same job openings you do. An acquaintance from a different industry (weak tie) has access to a completely different pool of job leads and professional connections.

**5. Maintaining Network Resilience**
A network composed solely of dense clusters without weak ties is highly fragmented. Weak ties hold the broader social media network together, preventing it from breaking apart into isolated, echo-chamber communities.

**Summary for Exams:**
While **strong ties** provide the **trust** and emotional support necessary for deep engagement, **weak ties** provide the **reach**. Weak ties act as structural bridges that allow fresh information, new opportunities, and viral content to flow freely across the vast expanse of a social media network.
