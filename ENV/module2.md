### **Q1: Write a short note on Global Warming as a Global Environmental Concern.**

**1. Introduction & Definition**
Global warming refers to the long-term, unprecedented rise in the average temperature of the Earth's climate system. This phenomenon is primarily driven by the **enhanced greenhouse effect**, which is caused by the anthropogenic (human-induced) accumulation of Greenhouse Gases (GHGs) like Carbon Dioxide (CO₂), Methane (CH₄), and Nitrous Oxide (N₂O) in the atmosphere.

**2. Why it is a \"Global\" Concern**
Global warming is not localized; it is a transboundary crisis. The emissions generated by one industrialized nation disperse globally, affecting countries that may have minimal carbon footprints. It is considered the most pressing global environmental concern due to its cascading effects on the Earth's subsystems.

**3. Key Detrimental Impacts**
* **Melting Cryosphere & Sea-Level Rise:** Accelerated melting of polar ice caps and glaciers threatens low-lying coastal cities and island nations with inundation.
* **Extreme Weather Events:** Increased frequency and intensity of hurricanes, heatwaves, unpredictable monsoons, and prolonged droughts.
* **Ecological Disruption:** Ocean acidification and warming lead to widespread coral bleaching. Many species face extinction as they cannot adapt quickly enough to shifting climate zones.
* **Socio-Economic Threat:** Food and water insecurity driven by failing crop yields will lead to mass migrations (climate refugees) and global economic instability.

**4. Conclusion**
Global warming cannot be mitigated by a single nation. It demands aggressive international treaties, such as the **Paris Agreement**, to limit temperature rise to well below 2.0°C (preferably 1.5°C) above pre-industrial levels through a rapid transition to renewable energy.

* **Examiner Expected Keywords:** *Enhanced Greenhouse Effect, Anthropogenic, Transboundary, Cryosphere, Climate Refugees, Paris Agreement.*

### **Q2: Explain Ozone layer depletion? What are the Causes, effects and preventive measures of Ozone depletion?**

**1. Introduction**
The ozone layer is a region in the Earth's **stratosphere** (about 15 to 30 km above the surface) that contains a high concentration of ozone (O₃) molecules. It acts as a critical protective shield, absorbing 97-99% of the sun's harmful Ultraviolet-B (UV-B) and Ultraviolet-C (UV-C) radiation. **Ozone layer depletion** refers to the thinning of this shield due to the release of chemical compounds into the atmosphere.

> ⚠️ **Common Mistake to Avoid in Exams:** Do not confuse Global Warming with Ozone Depletion. Global warming happens in the *troposphere* due to GHGs trapping heat. Ozone depletion happens in the *stratosphere* due to chemicals destroying O₃. 

**2. Causes of Ozone Depletion**
The primary culprits are **Ozone-Depleting Substances (ODS)**. 
> **Mnemonic Trick:** Remember the word **CHAC**
> * **C**hlorofluorocarbons (CFCs): Found in old refrigerants, ACs, and aerosol sprays.
> * **H**alons: Used in specialized fire extinguishers.
> * **A**erosol propellants.
> * **C**arbon Tetrachloride & Methyl Chloroform: Industrial solvents.

**Mechanism (Draw this as a Flowchart in the exam):**
1. UV radiation strikes a CFC molecule in the stratosphere.
2. The UV breaks the bond, releasing a highly reactive **Chlorine (Cl) atom**.
3. The Cl atom reacts with an Ozone (O₃) molecule, tearing it apart to form Chlorine Monoxide (ClO) and Oxygen (O₂).
4. *Note: A single chlorine atom can destroy over 100,000 ozone molecules before it is removed from the stratosphere.*

**3. Effects of Ozone Depletion**
* **On Human Health:** Increased exposure to UV-B causes skin cancers (melanoma), cataracts (eye damage), and suppression of the immune system.
* **On the Environment:** UV-B destroys **phytoplankton**, the foundation of marine food webs, which also act as major carbon sinks.
* **On Agriculture:** Reduces crop yields by inhibiting photosynthesis and damaging plant DNA.

**4. Preventive Measures**
* **International Treaties:** Strict adherence to the **Montreal Protocol (1987)**, the most successful environmental treaty, which mandated the global phase-out of CFCs and other ODS.
* **Alternative Technologies:** Replacing CFCs with Hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs). *(Note: HFCs are safe for ozone but are potent GHGs, currently being phased down by the Kigali Amendment).*
* **Safe Disposal:** Proper recovery and recycling of coolants from old refrigerators and air conditioners before disposal.

### **Q3: Compare natural and human-made sources of greenhouse gases. Which ones have the greatest impact on global warming and why?**

**1. Comparison of Natural vs. Human-Made Sources**

| Greenhouse Gas | Natural Sources | Human-Made (Anthropogenic) Sources |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)** | Volcanic eruptions, forest fires, animal/plant respiration, ocean outgassing. | Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas), massive deforestation, cement manufacturing. |
| **Methane (CH₄)** | Wetlands, swamps, termites, natural permafrost thawing. | Livestock farming (enteric fermentation in cattle), paddy/rice cultivation, landfill decay, fossil fuel extraction/leaks. |
| **Nitrous Oxide (N₂O)** | Soil microbial activity (denitrifying bacteria). | Heavy use of synthetic nitrogen-based fertilizers, industrial combustion. |
| **Fluorinated Gases** | *None (Almost entirely synthetic).* | Industrial processes, refrigeration, aluminum production (e.g., CFCs, HFCs, SF₆). |

**2. Which sources have the greatest impact?**
**Human-made (anthropogenic) sources** have the greatest impact on modern global warming.

**3. Justification (Why?)**
While natural sources emit vastly more CO₂ and GHGs by volume than human activities, they exist within a **balanced natural carbon cycle**. For millennia, the carbon released by natural sources was almost perfectly re-absorbed by natural \"carbon sinks\" (oceans, forests, and soil). 

Human activities disrupted this balance. By extracting and burning millions of years' worth of stored fossil fuels in just a few centuries, and simultaneously destroying carbon sinks (deforestation), humans have added *surplus* GHGs. 
* **The Rate of Change:** The environment cannot adapt or absorb this rapid influx. Pre-industrial CO₂ levels were strictly around 280 ppm. Today, anthropogenic emissions have driven them past **420 ppm**, causing the *enhanced* greenhouse effect.
* **Potency:** Human-made fluorinated gases (like SF₆) do not exist in nature, have extremely long atmospheric lifespans, and possess Global Warming Potentials (GWP) thousands of times higher than natural CO₂.

### **Q4: What are atomic and biomedical hazards?**

This question covers two distinct categories of modern environmental and occupational hazards.

**Part A: Atomic (Radiological/Nuclear) Hazards**
**1. Definition:** Atomic hazards refer to the risks posed by exposure to **ionizing radiation** (Alpha, Beta, Gamma rays) emitted from radioactive materials. 

**2. Sources:**
* Accidents or leaks at nuclear power plants (e.g., Chernobyl, Fukushima).
* Improper storage and disposal of radioactive nuclear waste.
* Mining and processing of radioactive ores (Uranium).
* Accidental exposure from industrial radiography or medical radiotherapy machines.

**3. Effects on Health and Environment:**
* **Acute Effects:** High doses cause Radiation Sickness (Acute Radiation Syndrome), characterized by nausea, tissue burns, and rapid organ failure.
* **Chronic Effects:** Long-term exposure mutates DNA, leading to severe birth defects and various forms of cancer (especially leukemia and thyroid cancer).
* **Environmental:** Renders land uninhabitable for thousands of years (exclusion zones) and causes massive genetic mutations in local flora and fauna.

**Part B: Biomedical Hazards**
**1. Definition:** Biomedical hazards (or biohazards) refer to biological agents or infectious biomedical waste that pose a threat to human health and the environment, often generated by healthcare and research facilities.

**2. Sources:**
* **Infectious Waste:** Used bandages, tissues, or cultures containing pathogens.
* **Sharps:** Used needles, scalpels, and broken glass contaminated with blood.
* **Pathological Waste:** Human tissues, organs, or fluids removed during surgery.
* **Pharmaceutical/Chemical Waste:** Expired drugs, vaccines, and toxic laboratory reagents.

**3. Effects on Health and Environment:**
* **Occupational Risk:** Needle-stick injuries can infect sanitation and healthcare workers with deadly blood-borne viruses like HIV, Hepatitis B, and Hepatitis C.
* **Public Health:** Improper dumping in municipal bins allows pathogens to spread via stray animals or water runoff, triggering epidemics.
* **Environmental:** Expired antibiotics leaching into soil and water bodies contribute directly to the global crisis of **Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR)**.

> 📝 **Exam Tip:** Conclude this answer by stating that both hazards require stringent, specialized waste management protocols—such as deep geological repositories for atomic waste and color-coded segregation/incineration for biomedical waste.
